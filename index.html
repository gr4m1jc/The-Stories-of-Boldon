Stories of Boldon

Brief History of The Boldons

Lying within the historic boundaries of County Durham, the villages are first recorded in print in 1170. Their names evolved from the words "Bold" or "Botl", meaning a building, and "dun", meaning a type of hillfort.

In 1866, work began sinking a pit that began producing coal in 1869; it was then known as Boldon New Winning. The village that developed nearby in the 1870s became known as Boldon Colliery. When the mine was deepened and extended in the 1910s, further housing to accommodate the workforce was built to the south of the pit in an area known as Boldon New Town.

Despite the relatively small geographical area, there are a significant number of older buildings considered as being of architectural merit including churches, public houses and former country houses, as well as modern additions. St. Nicholas' Church in West Boldon is a Grade I listed building; much of the stonework dates from the thirteenth century, but the foundation of the building dates from the Saxon period.

After the colliery was closed, the former industrial land lay derelict for many years. In 2000, Colliery Wood was created with over 2,500 trees planted; it is popular with the local community and provides a habitat for animals such as pheasants, squirrels, woodpeckers and otters. Colliery Wood provides six new paths, which are also suitable for cycling and link to East Boldon, and Brockley Whins and Whiteleas in nearby South Shields.

Notable people and Notable stories

Notable Incidents

The tales of Thunder Road:

With stretches of the road being constructed as early as 1600, Thunder Road (formally Newcastle Road) connects Boldon to the nearby town of Fulwell.

By the 1930s, the road became a paved route, beginning a long history of utilisation—not only for mobility, but as a drag strip.

Following national speed laws, in 1971, Thunder Road became a national speed limit road, and its future of pushing cars to their limits was set.

In 1995, 'Junior' was able to clock 140mph in his 1.1L car, after cutting a red wire and driving with both feet on the accelerator.

The golden age of Thunder Road ended in 2002, when its speed limit was decreased to 50mph, and then recently down to 40mph in some stretches.

According to legend, at 2AM, once you clock 100+mph, the ghost of Junior appears alongside you.

Notable people

Denise Robertson (1932–2016), television agony aunt on This Morning, who regularly appeared on the show from its inception in 1988 until shortly before her death in 2016, lived in East Boldon.

Daniel James Smith (1864–1911), miner who could allegedly lift two stones, both weighing approximately upwards of 43 kilograms, one in each hand. He lived in West Boldon all his life until a mining accident in 1911 - injuring him badly, leading to his death two weeks later. There is a memorial of Daniel in the back of Boldon Parish Church.

'Cooky' (1976–), born only mere metres away from The River Don - or the burn as many know it to be, he spent the majority of his early years submerged in its polluted waters. Leader of the West Boldon Wild Bunch (1987–1993), there was a notable instance in which Cooky was able to complete the 'Holy grail of digger jumping - The Leap over the well' in which he jumped over 12 yards, clearing the Don, and planting his soles in the mud on the other side - being the first (and last) in history to do so. Bypassers chanted his name, thus solidifying Cooky & the WBWB gang as a notorious group of digger jumping legends.

Matty Blah (1939–1945), was a young boy who had a pedigree in the destruction of televisions. One significant incident was at a friend's party, in which he couldn't help but destroy the £1500 television, using nothing but his cranium. Moments after the destruction, most of the party fled, out of fear of being accused in involvement.

The 'Boldon Brawler' (1950–1970) was a local boxer who was known for his aggressive fighting style and his ability to take on opponents much larger than himself. He won several regional championships and was a beloved figure in the community, often seen training at the local gym and giving back to aspiring boxers in the area.

The 'Boldon Bard' (1920–1980) was a local poet and storyteller who was known for his vivid descriptions of life in the Boldons and his ability to capture the essence of the community in his work. He published several collections of poetry and short stories, and his work is still celebrated in the area today.

Junior (1975–unknown) - broke the world record for fastest speed clocked in a car – 140mph, on Boldon's 'Newcastle Road'.
